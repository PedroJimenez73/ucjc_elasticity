3.3 Income elasticity

If ε >0 Normal goods

A normal good sees an increase in demand when incomes increase. Normal goods are also called necessary goods. An example is organic bananas. If a consumer's income is low, he may buy regular bananas. But if he has a few extra dollars to spend each month, he may choose to buy organic bananas. Other examples include clothing, water, and beer and alcohol.

Luxury goods, on the other hand, are not deemed a necessity to live. These goods are highly-desired and can be purchased when a consumer's income rises. Luxury items include cleaning and cooking services, handbags and luggage, certain automobiles, and haute couture.

If ε < 0 Inferior goods

Inferior goods—which are the opposite of normal goods—are anything a consumer would demand less of if they had a higher level of real income. They may also be associated with those who typically fall into a lower socio-economic class.

It's important to note that the term inferior good refers to its affordability, rather than its quality, even though some inferior goods may be of lower quality.

In economics, the demand for inferior goods decreases as income increases or the economy improves. When this happens, consumers will be more willing to spend on more costly substitutes.

If ε >0 Normal goods

A normal good sees an increase in demand when incomes increase. Normal goods are also called necessary goods. An example is organic bananas. If a consumer's income is low, he may buy regular bananas. But if he has a few extra dollars to spend each month, he may choose to buy organic bananas. Other examples include clothing, water, and beer and alcohol.

Luxury goods, on the other hand, are not deemed a necessity to live. These goods are highly-desired and can be purchased when a consumer's income rises. Luxury items include cleaning and cooking services, handbags and luggage, certain automobiles, and haute couture.

Inelastic goods

We speak of inelastic goods when quantity demanded responds only slightly to changes in price.
